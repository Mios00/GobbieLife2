01 — THE STORYBOARD PROBLEM

Numbers going up is the bait. The hook is revelation.

Candy Box didn't retain players with candy. It retained them with the suspicion that the silly counter hid an enormous, strange game underneath. GobbieLife is built on five engineered cravings — each one a reason to come back tomorrow.

▚
PILLAR 01

The UI is the progress bar

The screen is empty black with one goblin. Every bottleneck you smash physically re-draws the interface — tabs fade in, daylight cracks the warren, pipes and dashboards bloom. You're not grinding a number; you're grinding toward a transformation you can see.

↟
PILLAR 02

The most pathetic underdog

You begin as the single most worthless creature in fantasy — a blind lump of (._.) in the mud. The power fantasy isn't a chosen hero; it's vermin clawing up to rewrite the map. Rags-to-empire at civilizational scale.

◐
PILLAR 03

Tone is self-expression

At the outset the player sets the *voice* of their whole run — bleak and bloody, or gleeful and absurd — and it holds, so the narrator never whiplashes mid-saga. Two players' chronicles read like different games: screenshot fuel and replay fuel. (The slider up top is a live preview of both extremes.)

⌥
PILLAR 04

A saga that's half-yours

Authored tentpoles give the world weight and meaning. Procedural Whispers between them give every run a unique texture of names, deaths, and small absurd victories. The same spine, never the same flesh.

⑂
PILLAR 05

The ending is being written now

Tiny choices in Era 1 quietly accrue Alignment. By the time the four endings reveal themselves, players realize the finale was seeded a hundred clicks ago — and immediately want to replay for the others.

THE CORE LOOP, IN ONE LINE

Click → resource → automate → hit a wall → a story beat re-draws the world → richer click → repeat, three times, bigger each era.

02 — THE WORLD & ITS DRAMAS

A wounded realm of high fantasy — seen from the gutter looking up.

The grandeur is real: gilded elven spires, smoke-choked war-states of men, dwarven trade-holds older than kings. GobbieLife's twist is the vantage. You are the thing they scrape off their boots — and you are coming up through the floor.

The Gut, beneath the Dead God-Tree

Ages past, the high powers fought the Sundering Wars and bled raw magic into the deep earth. It pooled in the lightless mud beneath the roots of a god-tree they had already killed. The mud quickened. It learned to hunger, then to dig, then to want. That is you. You are not a chosen people. You are a side-effect — refuse that woke up. The whole world above agrees you are vermin. The whole game is proving them right, or proving them wrong.

The three powers above

The Sunspire elves — beautiful, decadent, certain of their superiority and quietly terrified of being replaced. The Iron States of men — fractious, industrial, already burning their own forests; your natural rivals and natural mirror. The Dwarrow trade-holds — neutral, mercenary, will sell a blade to anyone with the coin. Your first door into the world economy runs through them.

The question the game asks

When the discarded inherit power, what do they build with it? A boot? A wall? A market? A shield? The four endings are four answers — and the player spends the whole game, mostly without noticing, choosing one.

03 — THE HYBRID NARRATIVE ENGINE

Authored bones. Procedural flesh. A chronicle that's uniquely yours.

Pure procedural text feels like noise; pure authored text can't react. GobbieLife runs three intensities at once — ambient flavor, stakes-bearing events, and authored tentpoles — all gated by four state variables, so the saga always means something and always surprises.

◆ SAGA BEATS
AUTHORED · ~3–4 PER ERA

Fixed tentpole moments that fire at stage thresholds. Hand-written, fully branched on Tone and Alignment. These are the spine — meaning, characters, consequence.

≈ WHISPERS
PROCEDURAL · CONSTANT DRIP

Micro-events assembled live from authored fragment blocks — [WHO] · [DID] · [WHAT] · [WHERE] — filtered by Tone, Era and recent resources. Infinite texture, zero noise.

▤ THE CHRONICLE
PERSISTENT · SHAREABLE

Every beat and notable Whisper is etched into a scrolling Memory-Stone log — the player's personal saga, exportable as a wall of text. The replay & share engine.

⊞ THE FOUR DIALS
STATE · GATES EVERYTHING

Tone (grim↔silly, set at run start) · Alignment (4-axis accrual, kept hidden — surfaced only as Oracle riddles) · Era/Stage · Grit (morale). Every line of text queries these before it's chosen or assembled.

THE MIDDLE TIER · THE RECKONINGS

Between the flavor and the tentpoles: events with teeth.

Whispers tell you the world is alive; Saga Beats are the chapters you remember. Reckonings are the weather in between — disasters, windfalls, dilemmas and omens that draw from a stage-gated deck, carry real mechanical stakes, and are skinned by your Tone. They're what stop a long idle stretch from going quiet.

≈ WhispersCONSTANT

Ambient. No stakes. Pure texture and mood.

◇ ReckoningsOCCASIONAL

Real stakes. Often a choice. Logged to the Chronicle.

◆ Saga BeatsRARE · PIVOTAL

Authored tentpoles that redraw the world.

▼ CALAMITIES

Cave-ins and floods early; killing smog, tailings-bursts and ash-winters at the forge; great quakes, blood-plagues, starfalls and the Hollow tide at world-scale. The disasters escalate with the eras and punish overextension — the bigger you grow, the more surface they have to strike.

▲ FORTUNES

A rich seam, a prodigy runt, a fungus bloom, a defector bearing a Blueprint, a windfall of Spoils. Rarer than calamities, and never quite free.

◆ DILEMMAS

Choice events — a captured scout, a pack at the gates, a plague in the warren. Each option is a resource trade and a silent nudge to your Alignment; some are outright gambles (a good/bad branch on a coin-flip), and a whole strand — The Standings — turns each choice into warmer or colder relations with the four powers. This is where the four (five) fates are quietly written.

◌ OMENS

Lore in the wild — a Sundering-war relic, the Root-Priest's vision, carvings that hint at the Hollow. Rare, optional, and the breadcrumbs to the hidden fate.

ONE ARCHETYPE, THREE SCALES — THE CAVE-IN NEVER STOPS MATTERING
ERA I — "A tunnel collapses."

Lose 2–4 Runts and a cache of Mud. Grim: they're buried screaming in the dark. Silly: they're fine — just mortified, and very muddy.

ERA II — "A gantry buckles."

A Smelter goes offline, Scrap is lost, and Grit takes the hit as the town watches the smoke.

ERA III — "An outpost caves in."

You lose a supply node and a trade route — and foreign powers smell weakness on the wind.

DILEMMA ANATOMY · "THE CAPTURED SCOUT"

A Sunspire scout is dragged before you, terrified. The warren waits. Every door costs something and leans your fate:

Ransom+ coin, a Diplomatic lean, and the Sunspire owes you a favor.
Eat him+ Grit, an Aggressive lean — Tone decides how gleeful.
Free hima Heroic lean, and an ally who returns at the worst moment, for you.
Send backblindfolded — an Isolationist lean; the world learns nothing of you.
HOW THE DECK STAYS FAIR
State-reactive weighting. Low Grit pulls morale events; overexpansion pulls calamities; fresh tech pulls fortunes. The world responds to how you play — never pure RNG.
Cooldowns & draws. Each archetype is a card pulled from a stage-gated deck and set aside until reshuffle, so you never eat two famines back to back.
Doubles as the brake. Because calamities scale with your footprint, reckless growth carries real risk — the events themselves enforce the deliberate pacing.

05 — THE THREE ERAS · ENRICHED ROADMAP

Your roadmap, with the design connective tissue filled in.

I
The Feral Awakening
Pure black. Dirty, minimal, confined. Biology-based upkeep.

1.1 · The Lone Scrapper

100% MANUAL

One goblin, total isolation, a single button. This is where the Tone baseline is taught — the very first line of narration sets the whole run's voice.

+ DESIGN ADD: a 3-second cold-open where the screen is fully black and a single Whisper types itself out before the goblin even appears. No UI. Just the voice in the mud.

1.2 · The Runt Pack

UPKEEP LOOP

First passive income: Runts dig Mud automatically but eat Fungus. You hand-forage Fungus to keep them alive — the core tension of the era.

+ DESIGN ADD: a visible starvation state — Runts in the roster degrade (._.) → (-.-) → (x.x) as Fungus runs low. Loss is shown, not just numbered.

1.3 · The Mud Warrens

⌖ BOTTLENECK

Nav tabs fade in; Job Assignments let you balance Diggers vs Foragers to fully automate upkeep. Storage caps at 1,000 Mud — forcing the one-time Breach the Surface purchase.

+ DESIGN ADD: Breaching is the first Saga Beat — a hand-written moment where the pack sees the sky for the first time. Tone decides whether it's wondrous or horrifying.
II
The Iron Hunger
Smoke and fire. Rusty geometry, ASCII pipes and belts. Mechanical upkeep — Grit.

2.1 · The Smog-Pit

DISCOVERY

Daylight cracks the UI open. Two raw jobs split the pack: Scavengers pull Scrap (and drive Discovery), Woodcutters cut Blightwood — petrified deadwood. Discovery unlocks a chain of Blueprints; the first, the Char-Kiln, burns Blightwood into Ash (the universal fuel), at a small ongoing Grit cost (smoke). This is the only stage with a paid Lure a Runt button.

+ DESIGN ADD: Blueprints are physical finds with authored flavor — each Char-Kiln / Smelter / Forge / Mead Pit / Colosseum plan is a Saga Beat as Discovery climbs.

2.2 · Smoghaven

THE GRID

Discovery crawls now, and everything is built more slowly. Two mid-game unlocks matter most: the Embassy reveals The Standings (your relations with the four powers) and slowly mends them; the Hunting Lodge gives comfort + stage-scaled food (+1 → +2 → +2.5 per lodge across 2.1→2.3 as the hunting-grounds widen) to stretch the fungus. The Colosseum becomes discoverable here too (after the Embassy) as a huge prestige comfort sink. The Forge gains a Bank/Relight toggle — halt the Adamantium melt to hoard iron + ash. Every building's cost climbs steeply per copy — city re-planning and re-zoning — so growth is a series of hard choices, not a snowball.

+ DESIGN ADD: starvation has a short grace buffer then accelerates; a pulsing warning warns before the first death.

2.3 · The Ferrous Vault

⌖ BOTTLENECK

You draw the eye of the kingdoms — random enemy raids begin (out of a named faction), scaled by Notoriety. Answer with Defence: the Warband (+4 each) and two 9-tier structures rebuilt grander each tier — the Watchtower (→ The Brass Spires, 8→240) and the Wall (→ The Iron Horizon, 12→380); from Tier 5 both also cost Adamantium. A large gap between attack and defence multiplies the loss, split between Adamantium and population (weighted toward metal) — raids can kill goblins and sour the raiding faction's Standing. Or pay them off: the tribute's value scales off your Adamantium hoard and is randomly split across whatever resources you hold (priced against Adamantium). Discovery finally yields The Herald Horn (formerly the Iron-Clad Bastion, now 4× the cost) — sound it and the world learns the goblins have come; the note you choose is the first explicit Alignment fork — The Grand Maze (turn inward), The Ashen Conclave (a senate for all races), The Iron Legion (subjugate the world), or The Sanctuary (become the world's unseen guardian).

+ DESIGN ADD: from 2.3 The Hollow begins to surface — rare mythic Whispers and discovery events (ancient tablets, a breathing door, a welcome-frieze), each shown in cold cyan in the Chronicle, hinting a greater story lost behind the mist of time.
+ DESIGN ADD: the Adamantium hoard that makes you strong is exactly what makes raids bigger — defence must scale with wealth.
III
The World Blight
Brutalist dashboard. Heavy Unicode blocks █▓║. Macro-management and territorial control.

3.1 · The Ashen Dominion

SUPPLY CHAINS

No more single machines — Adamantium is now the empire's metal. You run external Outposts (Adamantium + Coin) and Trade Routes (Coin + Influence). Food is eaten super-linearly by Outposts and by every land you hold; Granaries burn Coin → Food. Claiming land costs escalate steeply, so expansion slows under its own weight. Unrest from ungoverned lands is suppressed by Garrisons (Adamantium) or the coin-fed Pleasure-Garden (bread & games — also warms the Splinter Packs); left to fester, high Unrest can spark an Uprising — a province revolts, and you crush it, buy it back, or let it secede. The Standings — a four-faction diplomacy engine (Sunspire, Iron States, Dwarrow Holds, Splinter Packs) — rise and fall with your Reckoning choices; let a faction turn hostile and it marches on you. A Threat timer rolls faction Wars, Natural Disasters (scaling with outpost count), and the escalating Dark Beyond (the Hollow, grander the nearer the end).

3.2 · The Iron Hegemony

THE FRONT LINES

The four powers stop watching and unite: a Coalition meter climbs with every hostile throne and every land you hold, and at full it marches on you as one host. Four levers of the Congress of Powers — Sway, Cow, Seal, Shield — hold it back and, quietly, this is where Alignment locks in (Aggressive, Diplomatic, Isolationist, or Heroic). Keep it low and the meter rewards you: below 20% the world is at ease and trade flows — up to +40% Coin & Influence, richest at 0% (each lever's cost also creeps up with territory through Era III, so it stays a live choice). Advance at 18 lands with your purpose certain. BUILT.

3.3 · The Grand Mechanica

⌖ THE CLIMAX

The final Megaproject — its nature set by your locked Alignment — is a two-clock race: pour Adamantium+Coin+Influence batches into the Mechanica bar while The Dark Beyond doom-clock climbs 0→100%. It no longer loops — at every 20% it launches an escalating incursion: resources → infrastructure → then it begins erasing land from the map (60%: −4 tiles, 80%: −8) — and at 100% the world is swallowed whole (a thematic Dark-Beyond Game Over). Finish the Mechanica and speak the final word (or Heed the Root) before it crests. BUILT.

06 — THE WEIGHT OF GROWTH

Three mud huts to a horizon of smokestacks — seven tiers, and you feel every single jump.

The moment you breach the surface, the settlement becomes a living panorama pinned to the top of the screen, redrawing itself as you grow. The way to make expansion feel heavy is to make each tier feel out of reach until it's earned: every step roughly triples the population and adds a new silhouette to the skyline. You don't read your progress as a number — you watch your town blot out more of the sky.

7 tiers across Eras II–III, shown by size-class (no proper names) + a live souls count that scales believably from a few dozen in the mud to tens of millions at the world-capital. Hit play to watch it grow — or scrub the slider tier by tier. PORTED & LIVE in the playable: a band pinned above the Era II/III viewport, tier auto-driven by stage + growth (no scrubber), population derived from pack size (Era II) and provinces + outposts (Era III).

That second kind is why two runs never build the same town in the same order, and why a rich player can still be stuck. A representative slice:

08 — PACING & THE ANTI-SNOWBALL

A deliberate ~7–8 hours, shaped as three earned plateaus — and engineered against the snowball.

My recommendation backs your instinct: keep 7–8 hours, but spend it as three plateaus, one per era, each sealed by a hard paradigm-shift gate that resets your sense of scale. The real threat to deliberate pacing isn't length — it's the snowball, the moment production outruns cost and the game starts playing itself. GobbieLife resists that on purpose, four ways.

LEVER 01

Coupled upkeep

Every producer adds a consumer. Runts eat Fungus; machines burn Fuel; outposts need Garrisons. Raw output can never run away from its own cost — growth always drags a bill behind it.

LEVER 02

Discovery gating

You cannot buy past a Blueprint or secret you haven't found. Progress is decoupled from raw numbers, so a pile of resources alone never breaks the curve — you still have to look.

LEVER 03

Hard ceilings

Storage and housing cap hard, forcing the next structural investment instead of infinite stockpiling. Each ceiling is a little bottleneck that keeps you spending, not hoarding.

LEVER 04

Grit as a brake

Overexpand and morale craters, throttling all output until you spend on Mead Pits, Hunting, and wins. Growth that ignores its people stalls — the settlement pushes back.

ON IDLING

Offline progress is allowed but capped at roughly 4–6 hours of banked upkeep, so the deliberate plateaus can't be skipped by simply waiting. The feeling you want is tending, not waiting — check in, rebalance, and leave it ticking.

THE NET

~7–8h for a first full run. The hidden ending and three alternate alignments push total content well past 20 hours across replays — without ever letting a single run snowball into autopilot.

Autosave, an export/import save-string, and capped offline progress. ✓ all three — the save-string doubles as backup and a seed to share.

02 · Can you lose?

A total Fungus collapse in Era I can wipe the pack to a soft-fail you claw back from. ✓ real stakes early, no rage-quit wipes late — later eras set you back, never end you.

03 · Number scale

Reject idle-game number inflation; keep values human (hundreds → hundred-thousands), never 1e40. ✓ yes — readable numbers are part of the identity and the deliberate pace.

04 · Tone — fixed at the start ★

Your override: Tone is chosen once at the outset and held for the whole run, so the narrator never whiplashes — continuity over mid-game fiddling. Still cosmetic-first, with flavor Easter eggs baked in at the chosen level.

05 · Meta & replay

No numeric prestige. ✓ a persistent Codex of unlocked lore, endings, and rare Reckonings — replay is driven by collection, not carried-over power.

06 · Sound

A Dark Room's lesson: near-silence is powerful. ✓ a sparse, era-shifting ambient bed (drips → clanks → drone), mutable, never chiptune.

Idle thrives on mobile. ✓ desktop-first canvas, mobile-respecting from day one — the ASCII must reflow, not just zoom, with real touch targets.
