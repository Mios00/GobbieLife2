# THE ROOT-TENDER'S SAGA

*A trilogy of the world after the Sundering — being the tale of the mud that learned to want, the horn that woke the thrones, and the door that was never a door.*

---

> *Before the ash there were two suns, and beneath the two suns an empire, and beneath the empire — holding it up the way roots hold a mountain — a god shaped like a tree.*
>
> *The empire is forgotten. The suns are down to one, and that one is tired. The god is dead, and lies across the deep places of the world like a fallen beam, and things live in his shadow that were never given names.*
>
> *This is the story of one of them.*
>
> — from the Codex of the Before, Relic the First

# BOOK ONE — THE MUD THAT WANTED

*Era of the Feral Awakening*

### The shape of it

A creature is born at the bottom of the world with nothing — not hands, not a name, not the concept of a name — and by the end of the book holds a torch at the roof of the dark, about to break the surface of a ruined earth. Book One is small on purpose: one warren, one hunger, one impossible ambition. It is the trilogy's taproot, and everything in Books Two and Three grows out of what is planted here — especially the things the reader will not recognize as planted until the very end.

### Part One — The Ache (The Lone Scrapper)

Far beneath the ash-choked world, in the drowned dark between a dead god's roots, a nameless slick of mud shudders — and, against all mercy, begins to feel. First the ache. Then the hunger. Then the slow, terrible wanting: to dig, to rise, to matter.

The opening chapters are claustrophobic and strange, told from inside a mind assembling itself. The mud-thing learns wet from dry, up from down, *me* from *not-me*. It scrapes. It hoards. It discovers that the black earth around it is not uniform — that some of it is sweet with old rot and some of it tastes, faintly, unmistakably, of *purpose*, as if the ground itself had once been asked to do something and was still trying.

Two mysteries are seeded in the first fifty pages and left to grow all trilogy long:

- **The knocking.** Something raps behind the east face of the warren — patient, spaced, deliberate. When the creature finally follows it, the knocking recedes, always a wall away, and leads it to a seam of rich mud like a reward. Or a lure. It never learns which. (It will, in Book Three. So will we.)
- **The dreams of green.** The creature has never seen the colour green. It dreams in it anyway — vast pillared halls of it, veined with light, and a sound like slow breathing that it finds, to its own bewilderment, *comforting*. It wakes from these dreams weeping mud, homesick for a place that no longer exists.

By the end of Part One the creature has a body it has half-chosen — squat, clever-fingered, big-eared, goblin — and, scratched onto a shard of slate in marks it invented itself, the first written word of a new civilisation. The word is a name. The name is its own.

*(In the telling, the name is* **Ketch** *— though every reader of this saga knows the name is different in every warren where it is told, and that this is the point.)*

### Part Two — The Stray (The Runt Pack)

Ketch is not alone. A shape detaches from the black and follows — the first of its kind to *choose* it. Then another. Then four. The pack begins in hunger.

This is the book's warm middle: the invention of society from absolute zero, rendered with the texture of myth. The first shared meal (pale fungus, grubbed from the wet dark, split five ways with a fairness no one taught them). The first argument. The first funeral — a runt lost to a cave-in, and Ketch discovering, holding the small cold hand, that grief is the tax on the word *mine*. The first *job*: this one digs, that one forages, and the warren eats because the balance holds. The economy of the trilogy is born on a mud floor between seven goblins deciding, solemnly, who does what tomorrow.

Threaded through it, the strangeness deepens:

- The fungus glows faintly along lines. The lines converge, always, downward — toward something under the warren that the goblins cannot dig to, because at a certain depth every tunnel curls aside as if the earth itself politely declines.
- An old blind runt — the pack's first elder, the first to die of nothing but *time* — spends her last season pressing her ear to the floor. Her final words: *"It isn't knocking, Ketch. It's counting."*
- And in the deepest gallery, the pack uncovers a root. Not a root of any tree that exists. A root the width of a feasting-hall, dead as stone, running down into the dark past all sounding — with a groove worn around its circumference, smooth as an old tool-handle. As if, over thousands of years, small hands had tended it. As if the tending had a shape, and the shape had been *taught*.

Ketch orders the gallery sealed. Not out of fear — this is the crucial thing, and the book is honest about it — but because standing before the root, Ketch feels the pull of a duty older than hunger, and is not ready to owe anyone anything. The warren has just learned the word *mine*. It is not ready for the word *ours*.

### Part Three — The Hoard and the Roof (The Mud Warrens)

Time passes in the compressed way of sagas — seasons of digging, the warren swelling to dozens, the invention of storage, of walls, of watch, of song. The Oracle enters the tale here: a runt born wrong-eyed, who speaks rarely and only in riddles, and whose riddles are always — awkwardly, unprovably — *true*. She is the trilogy's chorus. Her first riddle to Ketch:

> *"You were not the first to be lonely down here. You are only the first to be loud about it."*

The pack hoards mud the way other stories hoard gold, and the book makes the reader believe in it: mud as wealth, as mortar, as memory, the substance of the mother-dark itself, stacked and counted and sung over. A thousand loads, the elders say, and they can brace the great shaft. A thousand loads, and they can breach the roof of the world.

The last chapters climb. The digging turns upward, which every goblin instinct says is *wrong* — heat fades, damp fades, the dark thins out into something worse than dark. Whispers run through the warren: the surface is a story; the surface is where the dead go; there is nothing above but the Hollow wearing a sky-mask. The knocking, which has counted along the east face all book long, stops on the day the final shaft is braced — and in the silence where it used to be, the Oracle says her second riddle:

> *"When the door under the world was shut, little digger, it was shut from below. Ask yourself, before you open the sky: which side of a door do you bar?"*

No one understands her. The reader barely does. Ketch climbs anyway.

The book ends at the moment of breach — a fist of mud-caked knuckles punching through a crust of ash into freezing, alien light. The single sun. The ruined world. The bones of the empire of the Before, stretching grey to every horizon.

And in Ketch's other hand, unnoticed, carried up from the deep out of pure habit: a smear of warren-mud, the last of the mother-dark, pressed into the seam of the palm.

*The saga will hang on that smear of mud. But that is Book Three's secret to keep.*

# BOOK TWO — THE HORN OF SMOGHAVEN

*Era of the Iron Hunger*

### The shape of it

Book One was a birth. Book Two is an adolescence — loud, brilliant, ugly, and mortgaged. The pack becomes a town, the town becomes a power, and the power buys its seat at the world's table with a metal that should never have been made twice. It is the trilogy's engine-room: forges, factions, war-drums, politics. It is also, quietly, the book where everything goes wrong, in exactly the way the dead god's roots tried to warn them about.

### Part One — The Smog-Pit

The surface is ruin as far as the eye can see. Pick it clean.

The goblins do. The early chapters are picaresque and grimly funny: a people with no word for *sky* learning to loot a dead civilisation. They scavenge scrap out of collapsed manufactories, fell the black stands of blightwood — trees that grew after the Sundering and grew *angry* — and burn it in char-kilns down to hot ash, the fuel of everything to come. The refining chain is rendered like alchemy scripture: *blightwood begets ash, ash and scrap beget iron, iron and ash beget* — but that comes later, and the book takes its time getting there, because what comes later should feel like a sin.

The wider world arrives in embassies and knife-flashes:

- **The Sunspire** — the elves on their white mountain, led by **Lady Aelith**, who has watched the world end once already and corresponds with Ketch's warren the way a scholar corresponds with an interesting mould.
- **The Iron States** — the human successor-kingdoms, bickering heirs of the Before, whose clerk-envoy **the Tallyman** prices everything he sees, including people, including himself.
- **The Dwarrow Holds** — the dwarves under the eastern roots, led by old **Korrin of the Final Ledger**, who are the only ones who remember, dimly, professionally, what goblins used to be *for*. Korrin never says it plainly. He only refuses, always, to sell them tools that dig below a certain depth.
- **The Splinter Packs** — the free goblins of the wastes, wild cousins who regard Ketch's chimneys and ledgers as a fascinating disease, and their war-lord **Grosh**, who loves Ketch the way you love the sibling you will one day have to fight.
- And circling them all, **Uzgar Red-Maw**, warlord of the ash-wastes, the book's recurring storm — extorting, burning, returning, until his arc bends somewhere no reader expects it to bend.

Under the town, the deep tunnels curl politely aside, same as ever. The goblins have stopped noticing. That is the point of Book Two: *they have stopped noticing.*

### Part Two — Smoghaven

The town gets a name, and the name is a boast about its own poison. Smoghaven: shanty-rows and mead-pits, hunting lodges and ash-baths, a colosseum of blood and cheering, walls climbing tier over tier from wooden stakes toward black stone. Population is the plot now — every chapter the warren-born are outnumbered a little further by goblins who have never touched the mother-dark, who think mud is dirt, who were born under the single tired sun and find the old warren-songs slow.

Two threads darken steadily beneath the boom:

**The Relics.** Scavenger crews keep surfacing fragments of the Before, and a scribe-runt named **Fenwick** assembles them into the trilogy's buried history, one artefact at a time: a scorched banner sewn with two suns. A war-map of the Sundering, elf and man and dwarf drawn as three blades at each other's throats — and below the map's edge, small, in the mud, a fourth figure the mapmaker didn't bother to name. A child's primer from the green age, with a rhyme for remembering the ranks of creation: *sun, tree, root, tender.* Fenwick's growing codex says, in pieces the characters refuse to assemble: **the world was a tree, the tree was a god, the goblins were its gardeners — and adamantium is the metal that killed it.**

**The Chorus of the Open Door.** It starts as chalk circles in the shanty-alleys. Then unison humming heard through floors. Then workers who walk off shift and are next seen preaching that the Hollow beneath the world is not a wound but a *welcome* — that the dead god is a door lying across a threshold, and the hymn is the key, and everyone is invited. The Chorus is Book Two's cold undertow and Book Three's rising tide. The Oracle will say only one thing about them, and it costs her:

> *"They have the story right and the door wrong, and that is the most dangerous kind of wrong there is."*

And then the Forge. The chapter where Smoghaven first beats iron and ash into **adamantium** is written as horror disguised as triumph — the metal comes out of the fire not glowing, but *dark*, drinking the light around it, and every goblin in the forge-yard feels the ground under their boots flinch. Korrin of the Dwarrow Holds, when he hears, closes his ledgers and does not trade with Smoghaven for a year. He sends one line by courier:

> *"You have remembered the recipe. Pray you never remember what it is a recipe FOR."*

### Part Three — The Ferrous Vault and the Herald Horn

Smoghaven armours itself in its own sin. Raiders come for the hoard — the book's great irony, hammered home in fire: the metal that makes them rich is precisely what makes them worth raiding, and the walls climb tier by tier (mud rampart, brick, stone, iron-clad, and up) in an arms race against their own success. Notoriety becomes a resource and a curse. Uzgar Red-Maw comes himself, at last, and the parley between him and Ketch — two goblins, one born of mud, one born of ash, arguing across a brazier about what their species is *for* — is the moral hinge of the middle book.

It ends with the great work. Not a weapon, though every faction assumes so: a **horn**. The Herald Horn, tall as the watchtowers, cast whole in black adamantium — the god-killing metal shaped, for once, into a *voice*.

The final chapters are the casting, the raising, and the choosing. Because a horn sounds one note, and the note is a message, and the message will be the first thing the thrones of the world ever truly hear from goblinkind. Aelith sends a letter arguing for grace. The Tallyman sends a contract. Grosh sends a knife with a ribbon on it, meaning *whatever you decide, decide it like a goblin.* The Oracle, asked directly, answers with the riddle the whole trilogy turns on:

> *"Little king, the note is not for them. Sound it and learn who it wakes."*

Ketch climbs the Horn at dawn — past the fresh graves of the raid-dead, past the sealed door to the deep galleries where a groove worn in a root waits with the patience of geology — sets lips to the god-metal, and sounds it.

The note rolls out over the ash-wastes, over the white mountain, over the ledger-halls and the moot-fires. Every faction hears the thing it feared or hoped.

And far, far below — beneath the warren, beneath the curling tunnels, beneath the sealed gallery — something that has been counting since Book One, Chapter One, hears it too.

*It stops counting.*

# BOOK THREE — THE ROOT REKINDLED

*Era of the World Blight — and the fifth fate*

### The shape of it

Book Three is an empire book that becomes a parable. It opens at the widest the saga will ever be — provinces, coalitions, a hex-map of the world filling with goblin banners — and then narrows, chapter by chapter, act by act, to a single goblin alone in the dark with a handful of mud. Everything the trilogy has hoarded, it spends here.

### Part One — The Ashen Dominion

The Horn's note has remade the world's arithmetic. Goblin outposts stud the wastes; trade-roads run where the Before's highways lie buried; coin and influence flow into Smoghaven-that-was, now the seat of a dominion. The Standings — the saga's four-throne diplomatic dance — become open statecraft: Aelith's letters grow warmer and more afraid in the same ink; the Tallyman offers a *very* good price for something he will not name; Korrin's final ledger gains a column he has never used before, headed only *restitution*; and Grosh's Splinter Packs must decide whether the empire of their cousin is a homecoming or a cage.

But the land itself is wrong. This is the era of the World Blight: crops that come up grey, rivers that run warm and taste of iron, whole provinces where the ground has the give of something *hollowing*. Fenwick's codex, nearly complete, gives it a name out of the deep past — the Sundering never ended; it was only *paused*, dammed up behind the dead god's body, and the dam has begun to seep. The empire is expanding across a floor that is quietly becoming a ceiling.

Unrest ticks upward with every land claimed. Garrisons suppress it; pleasure-gardens distract it; nothing cures it, because — as the Oracle observes, watching a province riot over grain that rotted in a granary that was built correctly and stocked full —

> *"They are not angry at you, little king. They are standing on the same thing you are standing on. They feel it too. Anger is just the only door fear will use."*

### Part Two — The Iron Hegemony and the Congress of Powers

The world, frightened of the dominion and more frightted of the Blight, does what worlds do: it blames what it can see. A **Coalition** convenes — Sunspire and Iron States and Dwarrow Holds and even, agonisingly, some of the Splinter Packs, a congress of powers with one agendum: *the goblin problem*. The middle of Book Three is the trilogy's grand political theatre — swaying and cowing, sealed borders and shielded caravans, the four faction-arcs sown in Book Two all paying off at once. Uzgar Red-Maw's last ride. Aelith in person, at last, on a road with no banners. Korrin opening the *restitution* column and explaining, finally, in a voice like a closing book, what the Dwarrow Holds have owed goblinkind for three thousand years, and why he could never say so while it could still be mistaken for strategy.

And beneath all of it, the true antagonist surfaces. **The Dark Beyond** — the Hollow — begins to crest. Portals open where the Chorus's hymn was sung thickest: circles of cold void that eat provinces whole and leave scars on the map that no banner will ever fly over again. The Chorus processes toward them singing. The doom-clock of the last act starts to tick in earnest, and every court in the Coalition receives the same intelligence on the same night: *this was never a war between thrones.*

In the codex, Fenwick fits the last relic but one: the testimony of the ancients' engineers — the Before, in its final years, had known. Had begun to build, in secret, at ruinous cost, a machine to do with force what the dead god had done with love: hold the Hollow shut. A world-engine. **The Grand Mechanica.** Unfinished when the Sundering came. Waiting, in blueprint and foundation, under the ash.

The empire pivots on a coin. Every furnace, every ledger, every road and outpost and province of the dominion — all of it turns to one purpose: *finish the ancients' engine before the Dark Beyond swallows the world.* The hoard of a civilisation, poured batch by batch into a machine the size of a weather-system, racing a rising tide of un-sky. This is the two-clock finale the whole saga has been geared toward, and it is written like a bell tolling faster.

Except.

### Part Three — Heed the Root

Except that in the third week of the pouring, a glyph is found cut into the Mechanica's oldest foundation-stone — goblin-cut, warren-style, in marks descended from the marks Ketch invented on a slate at the bottom of the world. Fenwick cannot date it. It should not exist. It says, in the first written language of a people who were not supposed to have one:

> **THE ENGINE HOLDS THE DOOR. THE ROOT *IS* THE DOOR. ASK THE PRIEST.**

The Root-Priest is the last relic. Not a scroll — a person: the oldest living thing in the saga, found by the Wander in the deepest chapter of the book, at the bottom of the sealed gallery from Book One, sitting beside the great dead root with the groove worn smooth, *tending it still*. The confession that follows — the root-tender's confession, the codex's final entry, delivered face to face — reframes the entire trilogy in eleven pages:

The god-tree did not die of the god-killing metal. It died of *neglect*. The empire of the Before, needing timber and sky, cut its canopy and called it progress; the tenders — the small folk, the root-folk, the gardeners the war-map didn't bother to name — were driven down and down, and forgot themselves, and became mud. The adamantium only finished what forgetting began. And the Hollow beneath the world is not an invader and never was: it is the *absence of the god*, the socket of a pulled root, the cold that rushes into any place where a great love used to stand. It cannot be fought. It cannot be shut out by any engine, because it is not on the other side of the wall — it is the *hole in the middle of the house.*

The Mechanica would hold the door shut for an age, perhaps two. A magnificent, doomed clenching.

But a root, says the Root-Priest, is not a door. A root is a *living thing*, and the deep places remember how to feed one, and there is — there has always been, carried up out of the mother-dark in the seam of a mud-caked palm, pressed by pure habit into the first slate, the first brick, the first coin, the first horn-mould, spread through every made thing of goblin civilisation like a blessing nobody noticed — *seed*.

The last act is quiet, and it is the reason the trilogy exists.

The Coalition armies stand down, uncomprehending, as the goblin empire — at the very height of its power, its great engine half-built and the sky a quarter dark — *turns around*. The final pour of the Mechanica is cancelled. The hoard is not spent on the machine. It is carried, sack by sack, hand to hand, in the greatest procession the world has ever seen — goblins and elves and dwarves and men and the weeping remnant of the Chorus, who wanted a door opened and are given, instead, a garden — down the great shaft of Book One, past the warren, past the curling tunnels that curl aside no longer, to the foot of the dead god's root.

And there, where the story started, it ends. Ketch — old now, the saga's whole arithmetic written in scars on those clever fingers — kneels in the drowned dark between the roots, opens the seam of one palm, and gives back the smear of mud.

The book does not describe the World-Tree growing. It describes the *sound* — from far below, deeper than sounding, the counting starting again, except that it was never counting.

It was a heartbeat, waiting for company.

The Hollow does not roar or battle or die. It simply, over the last three pages, stops being an absence. Green returns to the world the way meaning returns to a word — from the inside. The single tired sun comes up on shoots of impossible colour cracking the ash from below, and the Oracle, ancient and smiling, speaks the trilogy's last line over the turned earth of the world's oldest garden:

> *"You turn from the sky and dig downward, back into the mother-mud — and the world above will never know how close it came, nor how small its saviour, nor how old the duty. Sleep now, root-tender. The counting keeps."*

## Appendix — The Fates Not Taken

*Every saga is a road with turnings. The teller of this one chose the Root. But keepers of the Codex record, for honesty's sake, the other endings the tale could bear — for it is told differently in every warren:*

- **The Villain** — the Horn sounds a war-note; the Mechanica is finished as a throne; the world is held, and hollowed, and his.
- **The Goblin Kingdom** — the Maze-note; the dominion walls itself in splendid, spiral isolation and lets the world above fend.
- **The Melting Pot** — the Conclave-note; the Congress of Powers becomes a parliament, and goblins are merely, gloriously, *citizens*.
- **The Savior** — the Sanctuary-note; the Mechanica is finished and given away, and goblinkind is loved, and the root stays dead.
- **The Open Door** — the Chorus's fate: the last hymn is sung, and what waits is neither dark nor light, and the tale simply… does not come back.

*But in the oldest warrens, where the floors are still mud and the songs are still slow, they only ever tell it one way. The way where the smallest people in the world are handed a sword, an empire, and an engine — and choose, at the end of everything, the trowel.*

— *Codex closed. The counting keeps.*
